NBA Finals History

75+ Years of Basketball's Greatest Stage

The Beginning

The NBA Finals has been the pinnacle of professional basketball since the league's founding in 1946. Originally known as the Basketball Association of America (BAA), the league held its first championship series in 1947, when the Philadelphia Warriors defeated the Chicago Stags. When the BAA merged with the National Basketball League (NBL) in 1949 to form the NBA, the Finals became the ultimate prize in the sport.

The Dynasty Era

The Boston Celtics defined the early decades of the NBA Finals, winning an unprecedented eight consecutive championships from 1959 to 1966 under coach Red Auerbach and led by center Bill Russell. The Celtics' dominance set the standard for team basketball and defensive intensity. Their rivalries — particularly with Wilt Chamberlain and the Philadelphia 76ers — gave the Finals its first era of truly legendary matchups.

Magic vs. Bird and the Modern Era

The 1980s transformed the NBA Finals into a national phenomenon. Magic Johnson's Los Angeles Lakers and Larry Bird's Boston Celtics met three times in the Finals (1984, 1985, 1987), reigniting one of the greatest rivalries in sports. Their contrasting styles — Showtime basketball versus blue-collar grit — captivated the country and helped grow the NBA into a global brand.

Jordan's Reign and Beyond

Michael Jordan and the Chicago Bulls rewrote the record books in the 1990s, winning six championships in eight years across two three-peats (1991–1993 and 1996–1998). Jordan's Finals MVP performances remain the gold standard for clutch excellence. The 2000s saw the Shaq-and-Kobe Lakers win three straight titles, followed by Tim Duncan's San Antonio Spurs dynasty and LeBron James carrying teams to Finals appearances year after year — including his iconic 2016 comeback with Cleveland from 3-1 down against the Golden State Warriors, widely considered the greatest Finals upset of all time.
