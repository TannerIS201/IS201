The Last Dance: Jordan's 6th Championship

How the 1998 Bulls dynasty ended on the ultimate stage

Posted by NBA Finals Blog on June 14, 2024

By 1997, everyone in the NBA knew the Chicago Bulls were living on borrowed time. Phil Jackson's contract was expiring. Scottie Pippen was underpaid and unhappy. Dennis Rodman was a wild card. And Michael Jordan, the greatest player in the world, had hinted that he would not play for anyone other than Jackson. The 1997–98 season felt like a farewell tour before it even began — which is exactly why ESPN's documentary later gave it its name: The Last Dance.

The Bulls stormed through the regular season with a 62-20 record, proving the doubters wrong at every turn. Jordan averaged 28.7 points per game, won his fifth regular-season MVP, and dragged a roster full of role players to the top of the Eastern Conference. But the real test was always going to come in June, on the Finals stage, against the Utah Jazz — the same team they had beaten the year before.

The 1998 NBA Finals

The Jazz were formidable. John Stockton and Karl Malone — two future Hall of Famers — had spent the entire season preparing for a rematch. Malone had won the regular-season MVP award, and many felt he deserved it. But Jordan had other plans. In a series that went six games, he delivered one signature moment after another, erasing any doubt about who the greatest player in the game truly was.

Game 6 in Salt Lake City is where legends are made. The Bulls trailed by three points with under a minute remaining. Jordan took over. First, a layup to make it a one-point game. Then, an improbable steal from Malone on the other end. And finally, with 5.2 seconds left, Jordan pulled up over Bryon Russell for a mid-range jumper — letting it fly, watching it fall through the net — and stood frozen, right hand still extended in a pose that became one of the most iconic images in sports history.

“I never thought about anything except making that shot.” — Michael Jordan, on the 1998 Finals Game 6 winner

The Legacy

Jordan finished the series averaging 33.5 points, 4.5 rebounds, and 3.8 assists per game, earning his sixth Finals MVP — matching his six championships without a single loss in the Finals. No player before or since has gone 6-0 in the NBA Finals. His perfect record on the sport's biggest stage is the defining argument in the greatest-of-all-time conversation.

The Larry O'Brien Championship Trophy — the ultimate prize every team plays for each June.

The Bulls disbanded that summer. Phil Jackson left. Scottie Pippen signed elsewhere. Dennis Rodman never won another ring. And Michael Jordan retired for the second time, walking away at the absolute peak of his powers. The Last Dance wasn't just a season — it was the perfect ending to the greatest dynasty in NBA history.
